# London Urban Knowledge — Architecture & Vocabulary

## Architectural Styles & Periods

| Style | Period | Key Features |
|-------|--------|--------------|
| Georgian | 1714–1830 | Symmetry, sash windows, brick or stucco, classical proportions |
| Regency | 1811–1830 | Stucco facades, curved terraces, ironwork balconies |
| Early Victorian | 1837–1860 | Italianate, stucco, arched windows, columned porches |
| High Victorian | 1860–1880 | Gothic Revival, polychrome brick, decorative ironwork |
| Queen Anne Revival | 1870s–1900s | Red brick + white render banding, oriel windows, gabled rooflines |
| Pont Street Dutch | 1880s–1900s | Informal name for Queen Anne Revival in Kensington/Chelsea |
| Edwardian | 1901–1910 | Baroque flourishes, red brick, terracotta, generous proportions |
| Arts & Crafts | 1880s–1920s | Hand-crafted materials, vernacular forms, tile hanging |
| Inter-war | 1919–1939 | Art Deco, Neo-Georgian, suburban semi-detached |

## Materials Vocabulary

### Brick
- **London Stock Brick** — yellow/buff brick fired from Thames clay, Victorian London
- **Red Brick** — darker fired brick, common in Queen Anne Revival
- **Gault Brick** — pale cream/white, Cambridge and East Anglia
- **Polychrome Brick** — alternating colours, High Victorian Gothic

### Stone
- **Portland Stone** — white/cream limestone, formal London buildings, Wren churches
- **Bath Stone** — warm honey limestone, Georgian Bath and London
- **Kentish Ragstone** — grey-green irregular stone, medieval churches

### Render & Plaster
- **Stucco** — lime plaster facing, painted, Regency and early Victorian
- **Pebble Dash** — aggregate render, inter-war suburban
- **Roman Cement** — early hydraulic render, Regency

### Roofing
- **Mansard Roof** — steeply pitched with dormers, French-influenced, Victorian mansion blocks
- **Welsh Slate** — blue-grey, Victorian standard
- **Clay Tile** — red/orange, Arts & Crafts and suburban
- **Lead** — flat roofs, parapet details, church roofs
- **Zinc** — modern flat roofing, also Victorian on some mansard roofs

### Metalwork
- **Cast Iron** — poured into moulds, lamp posts, railings, gutters — brittle
- **Wrought Iron** — hand-worked, gates, decorative railings — more ductile
- **Bronze** — statues, memorial plaques, door furniture
- **Copper** — domes, cupolas (weathers to verdigris green)

## Building Types

### Residential
- **Terraced House** — row of identical houses sharing party walls
- **Semi-detached** — pair sharing one party wall
- **Detached** — freestanding
- **Villa** — substantial detached, usually Victorian/Edwardian
- **Mansion Block** — purpose-built Victorian/Edwardian flats, often 4–6 storeys
- **Mews House** — converted former stable/coach house
- **Almshouses** — charitable housing, often historic Gothic or Tudor Revival

### Commercial/Civic
- **Public House / Pub** — often ornate Victorian tiled facades
- **Market Hall** — iron and glass Victorian covered markets
- **Arcade** — covered pedestrian shopping street
- **Bank** — classical columns, Portland stone, 19th-20th century
- **Town Hall** — civic buildings, often Baroque or Neoclassical

## London Mews

Mews streets were built in the mid-19th century to serve the grand terraced houses of London, providing stabling for horses and storage for carriages behind the principal streets. After the decline of horse-drawn transport, they were progressively converted into residential and commercial use from the 1950s onwards.

### Vocabulary
- Mews House / Converted Mews
- Former Stable / Coach House
- Coachman's Quarters / Stable Hand's Accommodation
- Cobblestone / Granite Sett / Cobbled Street
- Garage Door / Roller Shutter (modern conversion)
- Stable Door (split horizontally — upper/lower halves)
- Tack Room / Hayloft

## Decorative Arts Vocabulary

### Della Robbia Tradition
Glazed terracotta relief sculpture originating with Luca della Robbia (1399/1400–1482) in Florence. Characteristic features:
- White figures against cobalt blue ground
- Fruit and flower garland borders
- Tin-glazed terracotta (maiolica technique)
- Revived in Victorian Britain by Doulton of Lambeth and others
- Common on churches, hospitals, and decorative building facades

### Architectural Terracotta
- Moulded, fired clay — used decoratively on Victorian facades
- Red or buff coloured
- Used for: capitals, string courses, friezes, keystones
- Major suppliers: Gibbs & Canning, J.C. Edwards

### Key Terms
- Keystone — central wedge-shaped stone at crown of arch
- Voussoir — wedge-shaped stones forming an arch
- Console — projecting bracket supporting a cornice or shelf
- String course — projecting horizontal band across a facade
- Pilaster — flat column projecting slightly from wall
- Rustication — emphasised stone block joints, often ground floor
- Quoins — dressed stones at corner of building
